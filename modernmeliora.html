Modern Meliora

Background


What


Modern Meliora is a small business that sells botanical watercolor prints on her Etsy! This freelance project focused on setting up a Squarespace website to showcase her portfolio and shop.




Problem Statement


Modern Meliora needs a way to expand their business platforms that lets users view and purchase Modern Meliora's work in a centered spot.

Client's problem statement:
"I want to increase my online presence by establishing a website to showcase my work and increase my passive income."




Solution


By using Squarespace, given that it is the most beginner user-friendly CMS website, we will work together closely to bring her desired website to life using UX methods and research to make her stand out among similar competitors that also use Squarespace. The value it would bring to Modern Meliora and her customers is a community to build a relationship with these artists and bond over their interest in botanical watercolors.




Why


As Modern Meliora already has an Etsy shop online; it would be helpful to create a website that also shows her previous work that was already sold and is no longer displayed on her shop. With a website, customers can find her information beyond Etsy and view past or new watercolor designs. SEO will also help to increase online presence.

User Findings


Competitive Analysis


While Modern Meliora gave me inspiration for the website, I also used them as an opportunity to compare what their website offered. Two popular botanical watercolorists, Anna Mason and Shealeen Louise, both had unique features on their websites. I also decided to look at another general watercolorist, David J. Rogers, to see what made their website different from botanist artists. After combing through all three artists' websites, I organized the most common and unique features into 3 main categories: the home page, portfolio/shop, and what they did for customer engagement. Some of these features overlapped or appeared throughout the website, which appears in the middle between two categories. There were two features that David J. Rogers had that the other two did not: a search bar and FAQ assistant across the site. This would help users find what they would want to see compared to pre-set tags for a filter.

The most notable findings were that all artists had a section where they either separated their original artwork from other prints, having featured or related products on their homepage or prints details pages, newsletters, and class/workshop courses.




Sitemap


Next, I created a generalized sitemap of what Modern Meliora's website would include. Since Modern Meliora's goal is to begin establishing her online presence, she wanted to focus more on her portfolio and to keep it simple for now. However, I went the extra length and included shop so that way it would be built on UX research and save her the trouble of setting it up herself in the future. I decided to not separate paintings from original vs prints since she was confused herself on the difference, and allow users to access all paintings in one area within the site.




Wireframes


After doing some research in Squarespace, there was a lack of communication that arose between choosing a template. I went ahead and gave the client 3 templates to choose from. Ultimately, she went with Lusaka. I thought this was a nice, basic template she can maintain once the website was handed off. Additionally, I thought Modern Meliora could benefit from linking her artwork and shop in one easily accessible section on the homepage. I created lo-fi wireframes for each page. The most challenging part was not having access to Squarespace, so I designed "blindly" not knowing if Squarespace would have restrictions, such as certain hover states or column adjustments. I also went ahead and did mobile wireframes.

After doing multiple rounds of critique sessions and making sure Modern Meliora liked everything that she wanted over a few months, I found that sometimes what the client wants is not always the "best" for the business. For instance, she wanted two serif fonts on the website; I suggested that a serif font pairing with sans-serif would allow users to scan and read her website better. At this stage, I still did not have access to Squarespace, so it was still difficult for me to design accurately. The biggest obstacle was once I had access to Squarespace, I had to go back to the designs to modify them to make sure features functioned how she wanted or if it was something Squarespace could not do. This affected consistency across the site.

Conclusion

In essence, I had fun making a website for a small botanical watercolor business! I enjoyed helping someone establish their brand. It was challenging given that Squarespace had some limitations, especially with custom CSS code and functionalities. If I had more time, I would definitely dig deeper into Squarespace's analytics tool to measure what customers were engaging the most with! It would be nice to measure KPI's to help the business grow based on user research and patterns. It would also be interesting to see if I could implement a search bar or personal FAQ assistant similar to David J. Roger's website. As Modern Meliora continues to bloom in the future, I'd love to revisit and work with her again to add things onto her website that would benefit her customers to gain business loyalty.
